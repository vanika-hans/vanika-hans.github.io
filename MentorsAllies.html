Empowering Advice

So far, the biggest piece of advice I've gotten from Sai is the methods by which I can deal with stress, as well as her suggestions based on her experiences living alone in a brand new city. For instance, she suggested a logical approach to stressful situations, and after experiences a few of these after interacting with her, I really found it useful to break down stressful situations and divide and conquer the easiest first. As for living alone, she gave some tips how to survive doing this, such as things like always having background music going around in the background.

Sai has inspired me to not be limited in what the future holds by a degree. ELP Seminar Speakers

Jamais Cascio • Conference on World Affairs Speaker • April 6, 2016

Jamais Cascio is a professional futurist: NOT a predictor, as he may add. Cascio is an extremely unique individual, in the sense that not only is his career something I hadn't heard of prior to, but it involves a skillset that most, if not all of us, should develop to some extent. Cascio's occupation involves predicting and considering different possibilities that humankind could be faced with, in addition to laying out possible solutions. Interestingly, these "possibilities" refer to future conflicts, which aren't limited to physical possibilities, but also to common ethical practices.

The one that stuck out to me in particular was the discussion involving brain uploading. It wasn't so much this topic that got me, but after Cascio discussed the several points of views on this topic, I realized that there really is no direct answer to these ethical problems. There are thousands of possibilities for where humankind can go, especially with the level of intelligence that we have achieved in our current century. Not only is it then terrifying, but also extremely pressuring, that each and every decision we make affects our future generations.

To write this reflection, I purposefully waited as long as possible to see truly what the long-term outcomes were from this discussion. What I have determined is that Cascio's discussion really made me aware of who I am as an individual, where with the evolving world there becomes more and more ethical responsibility. However, with this responsibility comes the opportunity to further educate myself about each and every problem, in addition to hearing and taking into account other people's views on these opinions.

The "skills" I referred to earlier are exactly what Cascio's discussion instilled I should learn. The skills break down into the following. First, we all need to develop this aptitude to even question these difficult questions. MANY of the individuals I know tend to run away from issues in their lives, and avoiding these moral issues are going to be extremely harmful to our future. Secondly, we must develop the skillset to truly question everything: why do we think the way we do about everything? How can we change that? And, finally: we must develop the confidence and skillset to handle these issues, as well as the capacity to hear contradicting perspectives.

My takeway from this seminar had very less to do with what Cascio was discussing, but more so the holistic implications of the things he was discussing. Cascio's discussion really opened my eyes to where I am in my engineering degree, where all of the world I will be doing in just a couple years will actually be affecting so much more.

Danielle Feinberg • Pixar Animation Studios • November 1, 2017

Danielle Feinberg, a Boulder High School Alumni, presented on the intertwining of science and coding while working at Pixar Studios. Danielle specializes in lighting, which means that not only does her job title require an in-depth knowledge of physics, but also the capability to bring these concepts to life through programming and the creativity to make these features visually pleasing and effective in showing the audience a story. Danielle made it very clear in her presentation that her job in lighting isn't to recreate the world that we see, but to create a world that we can believe.

Danielle's discussion was very inspiring in many ways. What stood out most to me most was that her passion in life is so specific - lighting in animation. As a college student taking classes of many, many different topics, finding a specific niche of where I belong as an engineer is definitely a challenge. In Danielle's experience, although it may seem like a random specialty, she was only able to find her love for lighting in Disney movies by going out and learning about it. I aspire to be a leader in my life and go out to seek a passion that combines my interests altogether in a single job, just like Danielle who is using her love for art, computers, and science every single day.

John Holdren • Dean's Speaker Series • November 16, 2017

John Holdren has been the longest science advisor in history. Hearing him speak was very ground-breaking to me, as he made it a point to encourage those pursuing engineering to consider science/energy policy as a career, which is something I hadn't even considered. Holdren had started off as an Aerospace Engineering, and moved into the science/energy policy world years later. He claimed that his job was "never boring."

The 3 major responsibilities in his job were to manage:

  • "Science and Technology for the Policy" which entailed making sure that the president has information on what science and engineering say about the issues for economic policy, defense policy, etc.
  • "Policy for Science and Technology" which was working with the agencies and Office of Budget to plan out what the budgets are going to be for different initiatives, working with congress to appropriate the budget, and working to see how the federal government "can advance the ball" in regard to IP rights, immigration policy, arctic initiatives, energy, etc.
  • "President's ambassador for congress and countries around the world," which is pretty self-explanatory.

He also talked further about working with Obama, and claimed that "Obama was the most science-savvy president since Thomas Jefferson." He mentioned that Obama really understood that science, technology, innovation, and engineering matter for EVERYTHING (specifically all issues on the national agenda). This includes the economy, public health, climate change, energy, national security, and more. Obama was very interested in using Science and Technology to move forward. Holdren also mentioned that oftentimes Obama would ask baffling questions to the advisors, and send advisors off to figure it out. Holdren also talked about how oftentimes Obama would make his unhappiness well-known without raising his voice, which would "make you feel like you've let him down," which I found very interesting as many leaders can get loud when frustrated, but this sort of emotional disappointment actually says so much about how Obama connected with his staff. Lastly, he talked about how Obama was the type of leader to sit with his Nobel Laureattes; his relationship with his staff was not "corporate" or hierarchal.

Some current initiatives from the Obama administration (not sure what "current" refers to here) are: the Brain initiative and the Precision medicine initiative. The former refers to combatting antibiotic resistance, which in retrospect is a prominent issue effecting all kinds of medical advancements. The latter is to create an intersection between genomics, big data analytics, and public health records.

When asked about in today's era of distrust of the validity of science, "how can we get better at showing others the vital importance of science data to the society?" Holdren responded that we need to get good at telling stories about why science and technology matter. An example of this is when lasers were invented, the person who created it didn't know at the time that his invention would be used for eye surgery, showing videos, etc. We have to demonstrate good stories.

Dr. Mae Jemison • Leo HIll Leadership Speaker Series • February 27, 2018

I've never heard a woman speak so confidently about herself and achievements. Learning about Dr. Mae Jemison from the mouth of Dr. Mae Jemison was a very unique experience. She started off her talk giving us the following quote: "Don't Doubt, Dare" also saying that "It's not by being timid that we get places."

Jemison described herself with five different attributes:

  • Astronaut
  • Engineer
  • Entrepreneur
  • Physician
  • Education

Jemison described herself growing up at a time when so much was changing. During 1960 there was great deal of change regarding women's rights, social change, etc. She mentioned that there were "long, hot summers" after the MLK assassination. Growing up, she had a wide array of interests including dance, fashion, space, math, and archeology. After attending Stanford University at the age of 16 for Chemical Engineering and African Studies and Cornell for Med school she became an astronaut at NASA, and went on later to become the leader of the 100 Years Mission. She has even been an actor.

What I loved about her discussion is the fact that she called a simple cell phone a "space receiver." Is that not incredible? Something so basic that literally everyone in the tech world has next to them at all times is a piece of technology based off of the space industry technology.

An extremely significant part of Jemison's presentation was an exercise organized to determine the decisions we would make in building something new. For instance, in this theoretical innovation in regards to health, should we use 3D printed replacement parts or in situ rejuvenation (self-healing)? In regards to clothing, what fabric should we use? For technology, should we use silicon or steam punk? For Status enroute should we use Hibernation/suspended animation or awake generations? For food, should we use vegetarian or meat? For Work, should we use individual or assigned based on aptitude tests? After discussing this theoretical innovation for some time, the end point was that the challenges of interstellar travel mirrors the challenges that we face in the world today. SO POWERFUL. It was such a plot twist.

Some quotes that really stood out to me were:

  • "You don't have to choose one [interest], the breadth of knowledge is what matters."
  • "Even the sharpest blade can't cut its own handle." (Unless you have a wide range of skills, your usefulness may be diminished.)
  • Space is thinking about the way we can change our world, not just about rockets and propulsion.
  • "Coming out of college, you should still be learning and doing MANY other things beyond getting a good knowledge base.
  • "I suggest pursuing the extraordinary."

Others Impacting My Journey

An Unexpected Mentor

The word "mentor" has an authoritative connotation; a mentor implies someone that knows exactly what they are doing, associated with the "been there done that" mentality. I think of some old man with a long, white beard, who lives in a cave; he spent his life achieving the impossible, and now lives to advise others with reference to his path. To me, that mentor is not a true mentor, because if they have already reached the end of their path, they are on an entirely different level than I am. Their perspective is not relatable, because they have overcome the difficulties and now, with a calm attitude, reminisce. My definition of a mentor is someone that definitely has overcome similar experiences to me, but has also overcome these experiences recent enough to have a perspective that is relevant to me.

That being said, a very prominent mentor in my life has been a classmate of mine. This individual is actually an upperclassman in an entirely different field of engineering. Our paths crossed due to his ambitious pursual of an electrical engineering minor, in addition to an already rigorous mechanical engineering major. Thus, we met in a lab environment.

It amazes me to work with this individual, as regardless of the issues encountered, he diligently and confidently works to solve issues. There is no negativity, just a few jokes and then focused work. He does not even have electrical engineering classes to base his knowledge off of, just the singular prerequisite for the class and a great attitude. Not only does his work ethic fascinate me, but also, his willingness to help others is also extremely commendable. There will be instances where my third lab partner does not understand the hardware aspect of our robot, where he will have no problem jumping in and troubleshooting, and when I have issues with the simulation aspect of the project, he will also equally jump in and answer any of my questions. Somehow, he manages to keep a great attitude at all times, and with this attitude excels. I am extremely lucky to have him as a lab partner, but more as a friend. My mentor doesn't even know he is my mentor.

In the short time we have worked together, he has already taught me so much. I have really learned the importance of keeping a chill attitude and focusing on the larger goal than the small details. He has taught me the importance and advantage of jumping into tasks whether or whether not there is a plan. Good leaders should be humble, and through his selfless actions I have really learned how that attitude keeps a good dynamic. But, besides teaching me the importance of these leadership qualities, he has also made it possible for me to even execute these activities, as ideally stating what leaders should do is different than how it actually turns out in action.

As mentioned before, another aspect of this that has made him so important in my life is the relatability. He is a student, just like me, and yet his feats have proved him capable of achieving so much. I really feel inspired as a leader, because I know that one day, if I follow his path, I will be able to achieve just the same.

This has been important to me as a leader because I have seen exactly who I want to be in 3 years. After accumulating years worth of engineering knowledge, I strive to be an asset to the community in the way that he is. Given that I generally do not base my self-judgment off of my peers (but more of this idealistic version of what I can be), I am actually my own worst enemy; thus, this comparison, or more, reverence for another person is incredibly significant to my self-development, as both a leader and contributing member of future society.

Small Group Reflection 2

For this small group gathering, I had a great time seeing the progression of everyone’s project planning. It was very interesting to see some of the students discuss how they got through a problem they had talked about at the last meeting, and it was also great to see some familiar faces in ELP.

The main question/difficulty that I wanted to get some advice on was how to get people involved in joining a committee to plan the event. I wasn’t really sure how to incentivize it to engineering college students that already have so less time, and so some of the ideas I got were to advertise the committee as:

  • A resume builder.
  • An opportunity to get priority in deciding which company representatives come.
  • A free networking event – committee members don’t have to pay!
  • An opportunity to fill the Professional Development requirement for SWE.

This was very helpful, as I was able to get an idea of what people might look for when deciding to become involved with a committee. I will use this information to garner people to join the committee after the holidays.
